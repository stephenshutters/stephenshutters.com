What the Hell

  • 8th Dec 2020

On August 26th, 2020, I posed a question to my friends on Facebook and my followers on Twitter. Admittedly, it was a fairly loaded question. It was a question that I believe, when taken seriously, not-unduly challenges the long-held beliefs of us 21st century western evangelical protestant Christians. Rather than splitting this conversation into multiple comment threads by responding to each person’s comments directly beneath their own, I am attempting here to respond to as many comments as I can in one succinct essay form. My hope is that this will keep others from re-asking questions others have asked or making points that have already been made and also prevent me from having to respond to the same questions and comments multiple times. This is because I only have a limited amount of time in my life and am selfish with it.

My Facebook post and the multiple responses it received are pictured to the left. Yep, I know. That's a lot. If you're reading this on a small screen, feel free to zoom in on the Facebook discussion and establish some context for my following response(s), or feel free to just ignore that screenshot and jump in here headfirst. The question:

If Yeshua paid our debt by physically dying and not by spending an eternity in conscious torment, why would we think our debt is an eternity in conscious torment?

Also, before I get into the weeds with you all on this topic, I’d like to point out that I have zero qualms whatsoever with the ethics of the traditional view of Hell. I’ve believed in it for 24 years without issue, because YHWH is God and can do whatever the hell He wants. I don’t believe the traditional view of eternal conscious torment is unjust in any way; the Creator has every right to deal with His creations in any way He sees fit. Rather, the questions I have posed above have arisen not from any sort of trouble with perceived justice or ethics but from what seems to me and many other followers of Christ—both now and even as far back as the writings of Clement of Rome (AD 30-97), Ignatius of Antioch (AD 35-107), Polycarp of Smyrna (AD 69-155), Papias of Hierapolis (AD 70-163), Quadratus of Athens (c. AD 126), Mathetes (c. AD 130), Clement of Corinth (c. AD 130), Barnabas of Alexandria (c. AD 135), Aristides of Athens (c. AD 140), Justin of Samaria (AD 106-165), Theophilus of Antioch (AD 115-181), Athenagoras of Athens (AD 127-190), Polycrates of Ephesus (AD 125-196), Irenaeus of Lyons (AD 130-202), Clement of Alexandria (AD 153-213), Tertullian of Carthage (AD 145-220), Hippolytus of Portus Romanus (AD 170-236), Minucius Felix of Africa (AD 185-250), Origen of Alexandria (AD 185-254), Commodianus of Africa (AD 200-275), Cyprian of Carthage (AD 200-258), Novatian of Rome (AD 210-280), Gregory Thaumaturgus of Neo-Caesarea (AD 213-270), and Arnobius of Sicca (AD 250-327)—to be inconsistencies with the rest of Scripture. I am a definite proponent of tota scriptura (the belief that the context of the entire Bible must be taken into account for every claim and that cherry-picking verses is unwise), and it seems that the traditional concept of Hell as a place of eternal conscious torment is actually quite out of sync with the teachings of the Bible as a whole. I want to know what the Bible says more than what sounds pleasant or is even most popularly believed within any culture at any time. Goodness, this has been a long introduction! But I hope it clears up my intentions and removes from our discussion the potential issue of “You just need to lean not on your own understandings; trust what God says.” That is exactly what I am seeking to do.

First, I’ll respond to your claim, Aunt Jeannie, that “it certainly appears pretty clear in Scripture that [eternal conscious torment] is what is taught.” Interestingly enough, this traditional view of Hell did not become a standard within the church until after Augustine wrote City of God in around AD 410, in which he was the first Christian theologian to write a systematic defense of the view. For the nearly 400 years of Christianity prior to Augustine’s writings on the subject, the Church was actually pretty divided on it as can be seen by the several theologians I noted above from the first 300 years of the Christian movement. The first nine theologians I listed were actually around while the Apostles were still alive (the Apostle John didn’t die until AD 102). That fact in conjunction with the fact that the Church was divided on the issue for nearly 400 years suggests to me that, at the very least, the issue may not be so clearly taught and understood as many today often think. To go even further, however, I would argue that Scripture appears to more clearly and consistently teach the view known as conditional immortality (also known more reductively as annihilationism) than it does the view of eternal conscious torment.

Second, I’ll respond to your claim, Aunt Jeannie, that “Death in Scripture speaks of separation—like when God told Adam if he ate of that fruit, he would surely die that day—not physically, but spiritually.” You are correct that Genesis 2:17 says “. . . for in the day that you eat from it you will certainly die.” But the death mentioned here is a literal and physical death. The very word used here and in Genesis 3:3-4 for death is used in a literal and physical sense all other 79 times it is used in the book of Genesis and is even used to refer to the deaths of animals (and I’ve never heard of an animal experiencing spiritual death). In addition to all the times in Genesis when the word “death” refers to a physical, bodily death, the New Testament also uses the word in the same way many times. A partial list of examples being Matthew 10:21; 15:4; 20:18; 26:66; Luke 2:26; 22:33; 23:15; 23:22; John 11:13; 21:19; Acts 13:28; 23:29; 25:11; 26:31; 28:18; Romans 8:38 (which is a really fun one that says death cannot separate us from God and, therefore, challenges the idea of the word “death” meaning “separation”); Philippians 1:20; 2:27; Hebrews 7:23; 9:16; Revelation 2:10; 9:6; and 12:11. Now, the reason people take this phrase in Genesis 2:17 to mean a spiritual death rather than a physical one is that Adam and Eve did not actually physically die that day. However, this is a misunderstanding of the Hebrew idiom “in the day” and the special function of “certainly die,” which, along with attention to context, must inform our reading of the English translation. We must be wary of imposing our modern cultural assumptions upon these ancient Hebrew phrases. As Walter Kaiser states in his book entitled Hard Sayings of the Bible:

“It is just as naïve to insist that the phrase ‘in the day’ means that on that very day death would occur. A little knowledge of the Hebrew idiom will relieve the tension here as well. For example, in 1 Kings 2:37 King Solomon warned a seditious Shimei, ‘The day you leave [Jerusalem] and cross the Kidron Valley [which is immediately outside the city walls on the east side of the city], you can be sure you will die.’ Neither the 1 Kings nor the Genesis text implies immediacy of action on that very same day; instead they point to the certainty of the predicted consequence that would be set in motion by the act initiated on that day. Alternate wordings include ‘at the time when,’ ‘at that time,’ ‘now when,’ and ‘the day [when]’ (see Gen. 5:1; Ex. 6:28; 10:28; 32:34).”

In other words, the warning “you will certainly die” became instantly true in the form of a death sentence, conviction, or curse. It could actually be better understood as “you will be doomed to die.” In Hebrew, the phrase “you will certainly die” (מֹות תָּמֽוּת) is what’s known as an infinitive absolute, which has no exact English equivalent. It’s less about when death occurs and more about when the certainty of death is incurred. To confirm that modern Hebrew scholars have correctly understood the ancient nuances behind “in the day that you eat from it you will certainly die,” we can take a look at the Targum Jonathan (a second century Aramaic translation of the Torah), in which Genesis 2:17 reads, “in the day that thou eatest thou wilt be guilty of death.” This is also corroborated by other well-known experts on ancient Hebrew such as Robert Alter and John Walton. Alter states:

“The form of the Hebrew in both instances is what grammarians call the infinitive absolute: the infinitive immediately followed by a conjugated form of the same verb. The general effect of this repetition is to add emphasis to the verb, but because in the case of the verb ‘to die’ it is the pattern regularly used in the Bible for the issuing of death sentences, ‘doomed to die’ is an appropriate equivalent.”

And Walton affirms:

“The KJV, NKJV, and NASB all translate ‘in the day’ you eat from it. The NIV has replaced this with the appropriate English rendering ‘when.’ The expression ‘in the day’ is one of the major ways in Hebrew to say ‘when’ and does not suggest that the events described will take place within the next twenty-four hours, though, as Wenham maintains, its use implies promptness. More important, the verbal combination that describes the penalty (‘you will surely die’) needs clarification. The profile of this combination of verb forms (infinitive absolute coupled with finite verb of the same root) using the root ‘to die’ can be established with a good deal of confidence. In Jeremiah 26, the prophet delivers a scathing message to the people of Judah to the effect that the temple will be destroyed (26:4–6). The response of the people and priests is not repentance but antagonism toward Jeremiah as they seize him and pass sentence: ‘You must die!’ (26:8). The explanation is given a few verses later, ‘This man should be sentenced to death because he has prophesied against this city’ (26:11). Jeremiah 26:8 uses the same phrase as we have in Genesis 2:17, but it is Jeremiah 26:11 that shows us exactly what the people mean by using that phrase in verse 8. When they say, ‘You will surely die,’ they are talking about the eventual outcome of the behavior. The sentence will be passed, the doom will be fixed. This phrase is also used in the historical literature to pass sentence on offenders of various kinds (Gen. 20:7; Num. 26:65; 1 Sam. 14:39, 44; 1 Kings 2:37, 42). The resulting paraphrase of Genesis 2:17 then is: ‘When you eat of it, you will be sentenced to death and therefore doomed to die.’ Consequently, death will be a certainty.”

He adds:

“The significance of this has some impact on theology. The theological logic that has been applied to this text in the past is that since Adam and Eve did not drop over dead on the first bite of the forbidden fruit or die shortly thereafter (‘that day’), the warning from God must have referred to spiritual death. This is an excellent example of how exegetical shortsightedness can result in unwarranted theological conclusions. There is no reason to dispute the concept of spiritual death or to question that Adam and Eve’s sin eventuated in that condition. It is a mistake, however, to deduce that the warning of 2:17 had spiritual death in mind because physical death did not occur right away. In actual fact, the language of the threatened penalty did not imply that physical death would immediately ensue. This penalty was enacted when they were driven out of the garden and prevented access to the tree of life. Without such access, they were doomed to die.”

Simply put, before Adam and Eve ate the fruit of the tree of the knowledge of good and bad, they would live forever, but after they ate from that tree, they were then doomed to die. This is further demonstrated by YHWH’s words and actions in Genesis 3:22-24, which says, “The Lord God said, ‘Since the man has become like one of us, knowing good and evil, he must not reach out, take from the tree of life, eat, and live forever.’ So the Lord God sent him away from the garden of Eden to work the ground from which he was taken. He drove the man out and stationed the cherubim and the flaming, whirling sword east of the garden of Eden to guard the way to the tree of life.”

Third, I’ll respond, Aunt Jeannie, to your book suggestion. Erasing Hell was written by Preston Sprinkle and Francis Chan in 2011. Since the book’s publication, Sprinkle has abandoned the eternal conscious torment view in favor of conditional immortality. He has said on Twitter, “Conditionalism (aka annihilation) is among the clearest biblical doctrines that I’ve encountered in my 24 years of studying the Bible, supported by hundreds of texts and only challenged by 1 or 2 that could, in themselves, be taken to support ECT.” You can hear him tell the story of the change in his interpretation of Scripture here. In addition to Sprinkle's adoption of conditional immortality, Erasing Hell even says on page 86, “The debate about hell’s duration is much more complex than I first assumed. While I lean heavily on the side that says it is everlasting, I am not ready to claim that with complete certainty. I encourage you to continue researching . . .”

Fourth, I’ll respond to your syllogism, Kevin. I don’t at all disagree with you that Yeshua “was uniquely qualified to pay for eternal punishment.” I believe that He, in fact, did pay for eternal punishment—that punishment being an eternal physical death. What the Bible tells us is not just that Yeshua paid for our punishment, but more specifically that He took our specific punishment upon Himself. We call this penal substitutionary atonement. Romans 6:23 tells us that “the wages of sin is death.” And as J.I. Packer puts it, “With the other New Testament writers, Paul always points to the death of Jesus as the atoning event, and explains the atonement in terms of representative substitution—the innocent taking the place of the guilty, in the name and for the sake of the guilty, under the axe of God’s judicial retribution.” I’ve always been taught that Christ suffered the punishment that was meant for us, and that means that either our deserved punishment was an eternity of flames devouring our flesh and Christ suffered (and still is suffering) that or our deserved punishment was death (as the Bible tells us) and Christ suffered that death (as the Bible also tells us). Of course, the great news is that He not only took our death for us, but He also rose again from the dead, making clear that both life and death are fully under His control and giving us assurance of the eternal life we will live after being raised from our initial death. Now, I believe the direction in which you are headed, Kevin, eventually intersects with a point made by Samuel Roberts (and several others on Twitter as well) in a comment before yours. Sam argued by quoting Saint Thomas Aquinas that “a sin which is committed against God is infinite: because the gravity of a sin increases according to the greatness of the person sinned against (thus it is a more grievous sin to strike the sovereign than a private individual), and God's greatness is infinite. Therefore an infinite punishment is due for a sin committed against God.” This philosophy is often interpreted as proof for eternal conscious torment; however, I put forth to you that this line of reasoning doesn't prove anything other than the mere eternality of punishment. It is of utmost importance to recognize that both the traditional (eternal conscious torment) view and the conditionalist (annihilation) view agree that the punishment for sin lasts eternally. The dispute lies not in how long the punishment will last; rather, the disagreement is in what the punishment itself is. The traditional view argues that the punishment which lasts eternally is physical, bodily torture in fiery flames, while the conditionalist view argues that the punishment which lasts eternally is physical, bodily death.

Three main passages are used to defend this position (I may delete this section.):

“And the devil who deceived them was thrown into the lake of fire and brimstone, where the beast and the false prophet are also; and they will be tormented day and night forever and ever . . . And if anyone’s name was not found written in the book of life, he was thrown into the lake of fire.”

—Revelation 20:10, 15

“Then another angel, a third one, followed them, saying with a loud voice, ‘If anyone worships the beast and his image, and receives a mark on his forehead or on his hand, he also will drink of the wine of the wrath of God, which is mixed in full strength in the cup of His anger; and he will be tormented with fire and brimstone in the presence of the holy angels and in the presence of the Lamb. And the smoke of their torment ascends forever and ever; they have no rest day and night, those who worship the beast and his image, and whoever receives the mark of his name.’”

—Revelation 14:9-11

“These will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life.”

—Matthew 25:46
